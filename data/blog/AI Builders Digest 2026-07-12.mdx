🎧 No Priors — "Travel Through the Lens of AI" with Glenn Fogel, CEO of Booking Holdings

The Takeaway: "There is no such thing as a moat." Booking.com CEO Glenn Fogel argues the only way to win long-term is to continuously develop new services and understand what customers truly need before committing capital.

Glenn, a 27-year Booking veteran, joined the company when it was worth a few hundred million and helped grow it to a 00B+ business. His core insights:

- **AI is a beneficial tool, not a destroyer:** "The truth is we look at AI as an incredible beneficial tool. A business is to do something of value to its customers. AI, particularly using large language models, can help make it a much more valuable method for travelers to get information they need, and beneficial to our partners."

- **Complex travel is about to transform:** He predicts personalized AI agents that "know everything about you" and can rapidly search thousands of permutations — doing more than any human travel agent because "the machine never forgets anything."

- **AI and jobs — speed matters:** Unlike past industrial shifts, "the issue that's really interesting is the speed of the change." He cites the example of Booking's 40-language customer service, once staffed by humans everywhere, now all machine-translated. His concern: "the rate of job disappearance and new job creation are not happening at the same rate," and society needs to figure out what to do with affected people, like a 50-year-old truck driver whose job gets automated.

- **Don't sell too early:** When asked if founders should exit now, he says "it really depends a lot on what the situation is, how confident is management, and what are you really trying to accomplish? Are you trying to make something that matters, or are you just here to make money?"

- **Choose wisely:** His personal advice: "You only get one life. For people who have the ability to choose, I'd say choose wisely because you will not get that time back." He fears too many people who took convenient paths will end up "wistful about, gee, I wonder if I had done it next."
