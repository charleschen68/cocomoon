No Priors: Glenn Fogel, CEO of Booking Holdings

The takeaway: “There is no such thing as a moat.” Glenn’s core thesis is that no business area is immune to innovation — competitive advantages can disappear overnight. The only way to win long-term is to keep developing new services. He sees AI not as a replacement threat to travel companies, but as a beneficial tool that makes their core mission — connecting travelers with experiences — cheaper and better.

Glenn (a 27-year Booking veteran who watched market cap rise 1,000x from $6 to ~$6,000 per share) is particularly candid about AI’s impact on jobs. The issue isn’t replacement — it’s speed. Machine translation eliminated hundreds of human translation jobs at Booking almost overnight, but retrained workers ended up better skilled for other roles. His concern: the 50-year-old truck driver whose automated job disappears — society isn’t thinking hard enough about displaced workers. He believes retraining must happen within companies, not through government programs, which “hasn’t worked out so well over the last fifty years.”

His most memorable line: “You only get one life now. For people who have the ability to choose, choose wisely — because you will not get that time back.” He saw that fear in too many law school classmates who took the comfortable path instead of their real interests.

On the AI investment boom, he’s pragmatic: “I don’t know what the ratio of success to failures will be, but this is not new. Speculative booms create tremendous innovation. But there’ll be a great deal of disappointment — that’s just the nature of how our economy works.”

“AI is an incredible beneficial tool and a way for us to do our mission easier, hopefully cheaper, and better for our customers. All business. What is the purpose of a business? A business is to do something of value to its customers.”
